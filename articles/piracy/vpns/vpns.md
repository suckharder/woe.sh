# VPNs

## Introduction and scope

To Alice, a VPN could mean *"the thing I use to securely connect to work remotely"*, to Bob, a VPN could mean *"the thing I use to hide my IP and the data in transmission"*.

The underlying technology is the *same*. The use-case is *different*.

This resource specifically focuses on the **latter**.

## The raw truth

*Hypothetically*, if we imagine an **ideal** VPN provider, one you can fully trust, with no data retention - a VPN **could** be a good privacy tool. But - even in this utopian, **unrealistic** scenario, it would **not** be a miracle solution - there would still be a myriad of other variables in the equation of anonymity, ranging from technical ones all the way to the *human factor*.

Despite all this, a VPN *can* still *somewhat* improve your privacy, if you utilize it correctly. This also depends on your threat model. And if the threat actor is a *lawyer from Netflix*, it's a downright *reasonable* choice. As a rule of thumb, I would advise **not** to think of VPNs as privacy tools, even though all the advertisements you see online say otherwise.

**TL;DR:** No, your VPN won't protect you from the NSA. Yes, your VPN can protect you from copyright trolls.
